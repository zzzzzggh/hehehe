# PAC-51-R29F-META-R5H-GLOBAL-OPPORTUNITY-ORDINARY-PORTFOLIO-EVIDENCE-R1E

Date: 2026-07-20

## 1. Stage boundary

This stage retrieves only mechanism-level E2-E4 evidence:

- E2 — ordinary later-entrant revenue, effort or distribution evidence;
- E3 — failure, exit, concentration, support or maintenance-tail evidence;
- E4 — multi-asset portfolio economics, hit rate, decay and failure correlation.

It authorizes no named product, game concept, feature set, price, page, outreach, payment, KYC, prototype or build.

## 3. Evidence quality labels

- **Q1** — large cohort, academic study, official platform dataset or transparent methodology;
- **Q2** — industry survey with meaningful sample but self-selection or reporting bias;
- **Q3** — single-founder case, secondary analysis or mechanism-equivalent evidence;
- **Q4** — weak, promotional or unverifiable; may only document scarcity and cannot raise a score.

## 4. China domestic lane — 15 signals

1. **A01 / consumer subscription apps / E2-E3 / Q1** — RevenueCat's 75,000-app cohort shows newly launched top-5% apps earning more than 400 times the bottom quartile after the first year. This is mechanism-equivalent evidence for China consumer apps: automatic billing does not produce ordinary success.
2. **A02 / consumer subscription apps / E2 / Q1** — RevenueCat's earlier 29,000-app cohort found only 17.2% reached USD 1,000 monthly revenue and 3.5% reached USD 10,000. The ordinary entrant base rate is low even before founder time and acquisition cost.
3. **A03 / multi-app portfolio / E4 / Q1** — An Apple App Store empirical study found popularity spillovers between a developer's existing and new apps, but also a negative effect of portfolio category diversity on app quality. Portfolio reuse is real; unfocused proliferation has a measurable quality penalty.
4. **A04 / China indie app portfolio / E2-E4 proxy / Q3** — A Chinese independent developer's annual public report shows a single app operating across App Store, Google Play and domestic Android stores, with materially different year-on-year trajectories by store. Cross-store diversification exists, but platform-specific decay remains correlated with ecosystem policy and distribution.
5. **A05 / China games / E3 / Q2** — The 2025 China game report attributes growth partly to long-lived head products; the three largest genres accounted for more than half of top-100 mobile-game revenue. Market growth is not ordinary small-studio economics.
6. **A06 / China mini-games / E3 / Q1** — WeChat mini-game LTV research reports purchase rates as low as 0.1% in real advertising settings. IAP portfolios require large traffic, prediction and optimization; they are not passive catalogs.
7. **A07 / game production risk / E3 / Q1 mechanism-equivalent** — A study of 23,485 Steam games found 48% delayed release, with a median delay of 14 days. Production schedules are systematically uncertain even before market failure.
8. **A08 / Chinese web literature / E2-E4 scarcity proxy / Q2** — The 2025 industry report records 32.694 million authors and 45.837 million works against a RMB 50.21 billion reading market. Because market revenue is not author payout and is highly skewed, the data primarily proves extreme supply density and the absence of ordinary-author Portfolio economics.
9. **A09 / Chinese literature IP / E2-E3 survivor-bias marker / Q2** — Reports highlight rapidly increasing numbers of young authors earning more than RMB 1 million and a large IP adaptation market, while providing no denominator or median. Headline adaptation cases cannot be converted into ordinary derivative-rights economics.
10. **A10 / Bilibili creators / E2 gap / Q2** — Nearly 3 million creators earned some amount in 2025 and more than 10 million users paid creators, but no median earnings or livelihood threshold is disclosed. 'Earned something' cannot establish owner cash-flow density.
11. **A11 / creator platforms / E2-E3 / Q1 mechanism-equivalent** — Cross-platform earnings research finds power-law distributions; strong recommendation platforms have lower median and middle-class earnings than Patreon-like direct-support structures. Algorithmic distribution increases hit concentration.
12. **A12 / VTuber portfolios / E2-E3 / Q1 mechanism-equivalent** — A dataset covering 1,923 VTubers and over one million streaming hours found stark income inequality and increasing agency concentration. Continuous content and agency effects dominate passive-catalog economics.
13. **A13 / mini-program maintenance / E3 / Q1** — A crawl of 3.45 million mini-programs found 40,880 leaked master keys. Low-code distribution does not remove security, backend and maintenance responsibility.
14. **A14 / AI-assisted app production / E3 / Q1** — AppForge found the best evaluated model produced only 18.8% functionally correct complete Android apps. Lower coding cost does not eliminate system-integration and lifecycle defects.
15. **A15 / SaaS engineering complexity / E3 / Q1** — SaaSBench found more than 95% of agent failures occurred before deep business logic, mainly in setup and integration. Development-time compression cannot be treated as automatic maintenance compression.

## 5. China capabilities serving global demand — 8 signals

1. **B01 / Steam ordinary developers / E2 / Q2** — Analysis of approximately 44,000 Steam developers found only about 10% had ever exceeded USD 100,000 gross lifetime revenue. This is before taxes, platform share, contractors, publishers and development time.
2. **B02 / Steam publisher concentration / E3 / Q2** — The largest 250 publishers captured about 91% of annual Steam revenue. A non-winner-take-all market can still be winner-take-most.
3. **B03 / indie-game validation / E3 / Q1** — Interviews with independent studios found pre-release experimentation constrained by participant access, bias, time and resources. Cheap prototypes do not guarantee cheap market validation.
4. **B04 / indie-game distribution ownership / E2 / Q1** — A causal study found Twitter marketing associated with roughly 314% more release-week reviews and doubled the probability of reaching the ten-review threshold. Distribution effort materially changes outcomes and is not separable from production economics.
5. **B05 / stock-media catalog / E3-E4 proxy / Q1-Q2** — Shutterstock contributor rates depend on annual download levels and reset each January; Adobe and Envato allocate revenue by licensing or subscriber usage. Catalog size matters, but freshness, annual resets and low per-license economics weaken 'upload once forever' assumptions.
6. **B06 / indie-book catalog / E2-E4 / Q2** — A 2025 survey found about 80% of authors with one to three books under USD 100 monthly; authors with 25+ books had approximately USD 3,000 median monthly income and more than 40% earned USD 5,000+. Catalog size is strongly associated with income but requires a large backlist.
7. **B07 / indie-author distribution / E2-E4 / Q2** — A 2024 survey found 46% earning USD 100 or less per month; the highest income group averaged 61 books, while the lowest group averaged nine. Self-selection prevents causal interpretation, but it rejects the idea that a tiny catalog normally creates stable income.
8. **B08 / author portfolio cost / E4 / Q2** — Reported average monthly marketing spend rose from USD 75 for one to two books to USD 1,594 for 25-49 books. Portfolio revenue and portfolio acquisition cost rise together; catalog scale is not costless diversification.

## 6. Global projects remotely operated from China — 7 signals

1. **C01 / mature subscription apps / E2-E3 / Q1** — RevenueCat 2026 data shows top-quartile apps growing MRR by at least 80% while bottom-quartile apps shrank by more than 33%. Maintenance mode is not stable in a polarized subscription market.
2. **C02 / bootstrapped SaaS exits / E2-E4 proxy / Q2** — MicroConf reports 60% of bootstrapped SaaS exits priced at 1x-3x forward ARR, 37% sold with teams of two to five, and 20% of exits occurred at USD 1-3 million ARR. Small-team software can become a transferable asset, but these are successful-exit cohorts rather than entry distributions.
3. **C03 / selected SaaS portfolio survival / E4 proxy / Q2** — TinySeed reports 95% of its investments still operating. This is a highly selected accelerator portfolio and cannot estimate ordinary founder survival, but it supports the proposition that capital-efficient SaaS can have durable operating lives after traction.
4. **C04 / Atlassian app portfolio / E3-E4 proxy / Q1** — Atlassian aggregates the USD 1 million Forge incentive across all apps owned by a partner, while vendors remain responsible for support, migrations and framework changes. Multi-app revenue is recognized at platform level, but maintenance correlation is high.
5. **C05 / public API maintenance / E3 / Q1** — Large-scale client-API research supports Hyrum's-law behavior: with enough clients, most observable behavior is eventually depended upon. A narrow public surface can reduce but not eliminate compatibility obligations.
6. **C06 / white-label and channel licensing / E3 / Q2** — Current reseller research shows high-performing channel partners increasingly sell service-led optimization and managed outcomes rather than passive licenses. Repeatable channel access often imports implementation and support instead of transferring them away.
7. **C07 / creative-asset subscription catalogs / E3-E4 proxy / Q1** — Envato allocates 50% of net subscription revenue by each subscriber's use of an author's items, and Market sales can carry a 50% author fee. A larger relevant catalog can capture more usage, but platform allocation, tax, payout thresholds and continued relevance shape net cash flow.

## 7. Cross-mechanism findings

### 7.1 Portfolio scale helps only when assets share demand and maintenance

Evidence from apps and books shows cross-product spillovers and larger customer lifetime value, but portfolio diversity can reduce quality and acquisition spending rises with catalog size. 'More assets' is not itself diversification.

### 7.2 Static catalogs are the clearest E4 candidates, not yet proven winners

Books, paid apps, games and creative assets provide the strongest observable catalog arithmetic. Their ordinary economics still require many assets, and revenue is concentrated. They remain priority mechanisms for Portfolio modeling, not automatic project winners.

### 7.3 Development automation and operating automation are different variables

AppForge, SaaSBench, mini-program security and API dependency evidence show that faster code generation does not proportionally reduce security, integration, compatibility or support tails.

### 7.4 Distribution ownership repeatedly dominates platform fee differences

Indie games, course platforms, creator platforms and author catalogs show that owned audience, direct promotion, store discovery and algorithmic feeds create materially different outcomes for otherwise similar assets.

### 7.5 High headline markets coexist with low ordinary base rates

China games, mini-games, literature, creators and global subscription apps all exhibit this pattern. Aggregate market size and top cases are invalid substitutes for ordinary-entrant owner cash flow.

## 9. Action-value and stopping decision

Another broad 30-50 signal sweep would mostly repeat platform and concentration evidence. The remaining high-value unknown is narrower: convert the strongest E4 lanes into comparable portfolio arithmetic.

The next stage must be finite and decisive. It may collect at most 18 additional E4-quality signals and must stop after one wave. It must estimate, for each retained mechanism family:

- typical asset production time and cash cost;
- median or lower-quartile revenue per asset;
- revenue half-life;
- asset count required for positive pessimistic owner cash flow;
- marginal maintenance per additional asset;
- acquisition spend and distribution work;
- within-family failure correlation;
- founder hours after maturity.

If comparable arithmetic remains unavailable after the wave, the mechanism must receive an explicit uncertainty penalty rather than another research extension.
